Research Overview

My research lies at the interface of quantum dynamics, cavity quantum electrodynamics, and condensed-phase chemical physics. I am broadly interested in how quantum coherence, dissipation, and strong light–matter coupling work together to determine chemical reactivity, photophysics, and transport in complex environments.

Methodologically, I work on rigorous and approximate open quantum system approaches, including hierarchical equations of motion, quantum master equations, mixed quantum–classical dynamics, and path-integral inspired methods. On the application side, I study polaritonic chemistry, exciton-polariton photophysics and transport, and electron transfer in confined electromagnetic fields, often in close contact with ongoing experiments.

Quantum Dynamics Methods

A central theme of my work is the development and application of quantum dynamics methods for open systems in condensed phases. These methods aim to capture non-Markovian environmental effects, quantum coherence, and strong system–bath coupling on equal footing.

  • Hierarchical Equations of Motion (HEOM). Construction and efficient implementation of HEOM for vibrational and electronic degrees of freedom in polaritonic systems, including convergence analysis and interpretation of the hierarchy in terms of environmental memory.
  • Approximate Quantum Master Equations. Development and benchmarking of Redfield-type, polaron-transformed, and perturbative master equations to understand their range of validity in realistic parameter regimes.
  • Mixed Quantum–Classical Dynamics and Ring-Polymer Methods. Use of surface-hopping, Ehrenfest, and ring-polymer molecular dynamics to describe photoinduced processes and to provide intuitive pictures that complement more rigorous approaches.
  • Future directions. Extending these methods with tensor-network ideas and reduced-dimensional models to treat larger, more structured environments.

Cavity QED and Polaritonic Chemistry

A major focus of my doctoral and postdoctoral research is collective strong light–matter coupling and its impact on chemical dynamics. By embedding molecules or materials inside optical cavities or nanophotonic structures, one can form hybrid light–matter states (polaritons) that modify reaction pathways and energy-transport channels.

  • Vibrational Polariton Chemistry. Quantum-dynamical studies of how coupling molecular vibrations to cavity modes can enhance, suppress, or reshape reaction rates, including the identification of “resonant suppression” conditions and the role of dark-state manifolds.
  • Exciton-Polariton Photophysics. Microscopic modelling of exciton–photon hybridization in organic and molecular systems, with particular interest in group-velocity renormalization, dispersion engineering, and the interplay between coherent and incoherent transport.
  • Cavity-Modified Electron Transfer. Ongoing work on Fermi’s-golden-rule based rate theories for electron transfer in lossy cavities, aiming to unify Marcus–Jortner-type descriptions with microscopic cavity QED and to clarify under what conditions cavity modes can substantially alter charge-transfer kinetics.
  • Connection to Experiments. Many of these models are constructed with specific experimental platforms in mind, including Fabry–Pérot cavities, plasmonic structures, and microcavities hosting organic exciton-polaritons.

Several of these efforts are summarized and reviewed in my Annual Review of Physical Chemistry article on collective effects in polaritonic chemistry, photophysics, and transport. Please see the Publications page for details.

Photophysics, Transport, and Nanophotonics

Beyond chemical reactivity, I am interested in how structured electromagnetic environments and molecular organization control energy transport and photophysical response in condensed phases.

  • Exciton and Polariton Transport. Analysis of how disorder, vibronic coupling, and cavity loss affect exciton and polariton propagation, including the emergence of ballistic vs diffusive regimes and the possibility of long-range transport under strong coupling.
  • Nanophotonic Field Structures. Use of simplified models and classical electrodynamics to bridge between microscopic quantum-dynamics descriptions and realistic field profiles in cavities, plasmonic resonators, and metasurfaces.

Electronic Structure and Nonadiabatic Dynamics

To provide accurate input for quantum dynamics, I employ and learn a variety of excited-state electronic structure methods, together with trajectory-based nonadiabatic dynamics.

  • Excited-State Methods. Experience with CASSCF/CASPT2, MRCI, EOM-CC, TDDFT, and related approaches for computing electronic spectra, potential-energy surfaces, and couplings.
  • Ab Initio Nonadiabatic Dynamics. Application of ab initio trajectory-based methods to model photochemical processes and ultrafast relaxation in molecular systems, and coupling such simulations to model cavity environments.

Current and Future Directions

Going forward, I aim to build a research program that combines rigorous quantum dynamics, cavity and nanophotonics concepts, and electronic-structure theory to address open problems in polaritonic chemistry and condensed-phase quantum dynamics more broadly.

  • Developing unified theoretical frameworks for cavity-modified charge and energy transfer that incorporate realistic cavity loss, multimode structure, and complex environments.
  • Exploring connections between polariton transport and non-equilibrium many-body physics, including possible links to hydrodynamic or KPZ-type descriptions in driven-dissipative systems.
  • Integrating ab initio electronic-structure data with coarse-grained cavity QED models to make closer, quantitative contact with experiments on molecular and material systems.
