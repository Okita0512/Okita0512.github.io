My current research lies at the interface of quantum dynamics, cavity quantum electrodynamics, and condensed-phase chemical physics. I am broadly interested in how quantum coherence, dissipation, and strong light-matter coupling work together to determine chemical reactivity, photophysics, and transport in complex environments, which are often in close contact with ongoing experiments.

Methodologically, I work on rigorous and approximate open quantum system approaches, including hierarchical equations of motion, quantum master equations, mixed quantum-classical dynamics, and path-integral inspired methods. I also employ finite-difference time-domain (FDTD) simulations to study electromagnetic field interacting with (chiral) molecular systems in nanophotonic environments.

Quantum Dynamics Methods

A central theme of my work is the development and application of quantum dynamics methods for open systems in condensed phases. These methods aim to capture non-Markovian environmental effects, quantum coherence, dephasing and decoherence, on equal footing.

  • Hierarchical Equations of Motion (HEOM). Construction and efficient implementation of HEOM for vibrational and electronic degrees of freedom in polaritonic systems, including convergence analysis and interpretation of the hierarchy in terms of environmental memory.
  • Approximate Quantum Master Equations. (Variational) Polaron-transformed, perturbative master equations to understand their range of validity in realistic parameter regimes.
  • Mixed Quantum-Classical Dynamics. Use of Ehrenfest, surface-hopping, to describe nonadiabatic processes and to provide intuitive pictures that complement more rigorous approaches.
  • Ring-Polymer Molecular Dynamics (RPMD). Application of RPMD to study nuclear quantum effects in chemical reactions and transport.

Cavity QED and Polaritonic Chemistry

A major focus of my doctoral and postdoctoral research is collective strong light-matter coupling and its impact on chemical dynamics. By embedding molecules or materials inside optical cavities or nanophotonic structures (that constructed with specific experimental platforms, including Fabry-Pérot microcavities, plasmonic structures, organic materials, 2D materials, and so on), one can form hybrid light-matter states (polaritons) that modify reaction pathways and energy-transport channels.

  • Vibrational Polariton Chemistry. Recent experiments have demonstrated that chemical kinetics can be enhanced or suppressed by coupling molecular vibrations to quantized radiation modes inside an optical microcavity. This phenomenon is referred to as the vibrational strong coupling (VSC) enabled change of reactivities. This new strategy of VSC, if feasible, will allow one to bypass some intrinsic difficulties (such as intramolecular vibrational energy transfer) encountered in mode-selective chemistry that uses infrared (IR) excitation to tune chemical reactivities, offering a paradigm shift of synthetic chemistry through cavity-enabled bond-selective chemical transformations. I have been performing quantum-dynamical studies of how coupling molecular vibrations to cavity modes can enhance, suppress, or reshape reaction rates, including the identification of “resonant suppression” conditions and the role of dark-state manifolds.
  • Exciton-Polariton Photophysics and Transport. Microscopic modelling of exciton-photon hybridization in inorganic and organic (molecular) systems. Analysis of how disorder, vibronic coupling, and cavity loss affect exciton and polariton relaxation, decoherence, and propagation. For polariton transport, my research includes the emergence of ballistic vs diffusive regimes and the possibility of long-range transport under strong coupling, with particular interest in group-velocity renormalization, dispersion engineering, and the role of quantum coherence.
  • Cavity-Modified Electron Transfer. Fermi's-golden-rule based rate theories for nonadiabatic electron transfer in confined environments, aiming to unify Marcus-Jortner-type descriptions with microscopic cavity QED and to clarify under what conditions cavity modes can substantially alter charge-transfer kinetics.
  • Nanophotonic Field Structures. Use of simplified models and classical electrodynamics simulations (such as the FDTD approach) to bridge between microscopic quantum-dynamics descriptions and realistic field profiles in cavities, plasmonic resonators, and metasurfaces, with particular interest in chirality.
